// Live Field Evidence — April 2026

The "Hello World" Problem

During research for this episode, 9 active Sri Lankan .gov.lk websites were found displaying WordPress's default "Hello World!" post — a telltale sign of sites that were either wiped by attackers, freshly reinstalled, or completely abandoned with no content ever added. These are live government websites serving citizens today.

🚨
What does "Hello World!" mean on a government site?
WordPress auto-creates a "Hello World!" post when first installed. Finding it on a live .gov.lk domain means the site has been reset, wiped, or was never properly built — all of which expose citizens and government data to serious risk.

Island Under Siege — Full Episode

podcast_script.md — ~18 min runtime
00:00 — Intro
🎙️ Cold Open
[SFX: ambient keyboard clicks, low hum, sharp alert beep]

Picture this. It's a Tuesday morning. A pensioner in Colombo logs into the government portal to check her monthly payment. The site is down. Meanwhile, 617 gigabytes of data — her name, her NIC number, her pension records — are already sitting on a dark web leak site, posted by a ransomware gang called Cloak.

She doesn't know. The government barely knows. And Sri Lanka's cybersecurity laws? Still not fully switched on.

Welcome to CyberPod. This is "Island Under Siege — Sri Lanka's Cyber Future."
02:00 — Segment 1
📊 Setting the Scene
Between December 2022 and April 2026 — just over three years — we've documented 26 named cybersecurity incidents tied to Sri Lankan organisations. Eighteen confirmed. Eight claim-grade. And we have 16 dark web screenshots — actual captures from BreachForums, ransomware portals, and underground data markets — to prove it.

Sri Lanka CERT recorded over 12,650 complaints in 2025 alone. Kaspersky detected over 9.15 million internet-borne threats targeting Sri Lankan devices in 2025 — putting us 20th globally for web-borne cyber risk.

And during the research for this episode, we found something even more alarming. Nine active government .gov.lk websites — including the Atomic Energy Board and two Ministry of Health sites — displaying WordPress's default "Hello World!" post. Sites that were either wiped, reset, or abandoned entirely.
04:30 — Segment 2
⏮️ The Early Days: 2020–2022
May 2020 — Sri Lanka Telecom. REvil attempted intrusion — contained, but a warning shot.

February 2021 — LK Domain Registry. DNS records changed. Government sites silently redirected. A demonstration of control.

April 2022 — PayHere. 1.5 million records and 65 gigabytes of payment data exposed. 36 hours of outage.

December 2022 — Ministry of Education. An actor called 'kelvinsecurity' lists 12,398 files and admin login access on a hacker forum — 10.2 gigabytes of government education data for sale. And we have the screenshot to prove it.

The pattern in this era: defacement, redirection, data theft. That was about to change.
08:00 — Segment 3
⚡ The Turning Point: 2023
September 2023. Ransomware hits the gov.lk email system. ICTA confirmed: 5,000 government email accounts affected. Three months of government communications — gone.

Why? Microsoft Exchange 2013 (end-of-life since 2018) and no offline backups. For a national government email system. This is like a bank vault with one key and no alarm.
10:30 — Segment 4
🌪️ The Storm: 2025
March 2025 — Cargills Bank. 1.9 terabytes, 1M+ files exfiltrated by Hunters International.

May 2025 — Department of Pensions. Cloak ransomware. 617 gigabytes of pensioner data posted publicly on a dark web portal. 392 views. We have that screenshot.

May 2025 — VS One Technology. Nova ransomware. 20 gigabytes. Countdown timer ticking.

June 2025 — NWSDB. SMS gateway hijacked. Bitcoin extortion messages sent from the water board's own trusted number.

August 2025 — Ministry of Health. A GOD-status user called 'Kazu' lists 398,769 patient records from health.gov.lk for $300. Ransom demand: $3,000. No government response. We have two separate screenshots of that listing.

September 2025 — Ministry of Finance. And then the most alarming: someone lists the entire Ministry of Finance database AND full RDP and SSH root access to core government servers for $4,000 in Bitcoin. Plain text passwords. Domain admin privileges. Data on thousands of government officials. We have the forum post screenshot.
12:30 — Segment 5
🌐 The "Hello World" Problem — Live Evidence
But here's something we found that isn't in any threat report. During research for this episode, we manually checked a cross-section of .gov.lk websites. What we found was extraordinary.

Nine government websites — including the Sri Lanka Atomic Energy Board, the RTI Commission, two Ministry of Health subdomains, and provincial government sites — were all showing WordPress's default "Hello World!" post.

That's the page WordPress shows when you first install it and haven't built anything yet. On a live government domain, it means one of three things: the site was wiped by an attacker, it was reset and never rebuilt, or it was never properly built in the first place.

The most alarming case: aeb.gov.lk — the Atomic Energy Board — had the word "oka" appended to the default post. Someone with admin access to that WordPress installation typed into it. And rti.gov.lk — the Right to Information Commission — was running without HTTPS, flagged "Not Secure" by the browser, while showing the same default page.

This is not a sophisticated nation-state attack. This is basic negligence at scale.
14:30 — Segment 6
🔍 The Pattern
The same failures, again and again:

🔴 Unpatched software — Exchange 2013, outdated plugins, stale WordPress installs
🔴 No backups — or backups on the same compromised network
🔴 Abandoned infrastructure — sites in default state for years
🔴 No HTTPS — government sites running over plain HTTP in 2026
🔴 No asset lifecycle management — nobody asking: who owns this? when was it last updated?

And attackers have noticed. From local defacement groups in 2020–2022 to international ransomware-as-a-service operations and underground data brokers by 2025–2026.
16:00 — Segment 7
🛡️ Policy & The Future
The Personal Data Protection Act (2022), Data Protection Authority (2023), and National Cyber Security Strategy 2025–2029 (Cabinet-approved July 2025) show genuine intent.

But the gap between law on paper and a patched government server remains wide. Sri Lanka has the laws. It has the strategy. The race now is between implementing those protections and the next attacker who finds a forgotten server — or a government website still showing "Hello World."
18:00 — Outro
🎙️ Closing
That pensioner in Colombo? She got her payment eventually. But somewhere on the dark web, her data is still there. A farmer in Anuradhapura? His NIC number is in a leaked database of 1.45 million records. And nine government websites are still showing "Hello World."

This is CyberPod. Stay curious, stay secure.
